SHEET 01

Abstract

A semantic search engine over a corpus of 140 architectural interior renders, built to find the exact edge of what a joint text–image embedding model actually understands.

This project uses CLIP (ViT-B/32) to embed a personal corpus of architectural renders and retrieve images by free-text query, cosine similarity, and a tunable top‑k. No captioning layer sits between the query and the image — retrieval is pure joint‑embedding, which makes it possible to isolate exactly what this class of model sees, counts, negates, and locates, before a language model has a chance to paper over the gaps.

The deliverable is a working Gradio search prototype and a ten‑entry retrieval atlas: documented queries, paired with what came back, paired with an explanation grounded in how embedding‑space similarity actually works. Nine of the ten entries are failures. That imbalance is treated as a finding, not an embarrassment — see Sheet 05.

SHEET 02

Motivation & corpus

The corpus is a set of architectural interior renders produced during my work in an architecture role — bathrooms, bedrooms, living spaces, dining areas, rendered during early‑stage design exploration for real client projects, then used in concept pitches, client presentations, and internal design reviews.

I chose this corpus because I know it better than any public dataset could let me know it. I know exactly what material each counter is, whether a chair is a real material or a render approximation, and which images are near‑duplicates from the same design pass with a lighting or angle change. That insider knowledge is what let me catch CLIP's mistakes immediately — when it called a beige counter "white," or matched a scene by room type while ignoring the specific object I actually asked for.

The corpus includes a small number of near‑duplicate clusters — the same room shot again with a lighting or framing change — left in rather than manually removed. I didn't end up querying them as a deliberate stability test; they're simply part of the archive as it exists.

SHEET 03

Approach

Level 1 — The Finder: joint embedding, no captioning, no generation. Everything the system knows comes from where an image and a phrase land in the same vector space.

SurfaceGradio interface,

Images are embedded once and cached; a query is embedded into the same space at request time, and results are ranked by cosine similarity with an adjustable top‑k and similarity threshold. My work was corpus curation, query design across ten documented cases, and threshold/top‑k tuning — not the embedding loop itself, which came from the course starter notebook.

SHEET 04

Retrieval atlas

Ten documented queries. Each card is a redline: the query, the verdict, and the mechanism — why the embedding space produced this result, not just that it did.

ENTRY 01Strong — 3× replicated

Bag-of-words material binding

“white bathroom sink” · “wooden dining chairs” · “wooden dining table”

Wrong materials consistently outranked the correct ones — glass and marble tables beat the actual wooden one. CLIP matches "wooden" and "table" as loose, separable tokens rather than binding the attribute to the object, consistent with the ARO benchmark's findings on compositional failure.

insert contact sheet
ENTRY 02Strong

Negation is ignored

“no television” · “no windows”

Both queries returned rooms with the negated object present and prominent. CLIP's contrastive training never taught it what absence looks like — "no television" and "television" pull toward the same region of embedding space.

insert contact sheet
ENTRY 03Strong

No counting, just presence

“two lamp shades” · “one lamp”

Both queries resolved to object‑presence matching rather than a count. A room with three lamps scored as well as a room with one for the "one lamp" query — the number is decoration on the prompt, not a constraint the model applies.

insert contact sheet
ENTRY 04Strong

Phrasing sensitivity vs. top-k

“plant” → “plants near pool”

A one‑letter, one‑phrase change moved the true match from rank 7 to rank 1. At the original phrasing, the correct image stayed unranked even at top‑k=6 — a small wording shift produces a large, non‑linear jump in the embedding neighborhood.

ENTRY 05Moderate — conditional success

Object salience decides reliability

“staircase” / “tree” (clean) vs. “fan” / “woman” (unclean)

Large, frame‑dominant objects retrieved cleanly; small or peripheral ones did not. Reliability tracks how much of the frame the object occupies, not how simple the query text is — the one genuine success in this atlas is conditional on scale, not on asking a clean question.

ENTRY 06Strong

Confidence ≠ correctness

“toilet” vs. “bathroom”

Both queries returned the identical six images, but "bathroom" scored roughly 0.02–0.03 higher across the board — almost certainly a training‑frequency effect, not a difference in what was actually found. Similarity score is a measure of familiarity to the model, not of retrieval quality.

insert contact sheet
ENTRY 07Strong

Threshold instability

“bathroom” at similarity 0.29 / 0.28 / 0.27

Result count jumped 1 → 5 → 9 images across a threshold shift of 0.02. The similarity landscape isn't a clean cliff between relevant and irrelevant — it's a shallow slope, and the threshold you pick is doing more work than the query text.

insert contact sheet
ENTRY 08Strong

Spatial relationships aren't seen

“book on bed” vs. “book next to bed”

Identical top result for both queries, and visual inspection confirmed it genuinely showed neither exact spatial relation — ruling out a lucky false positive as the explanation. CLIP embeds "book" and "bed" as co‑occurring concepts with no encoding of the preposition between them.

insert contact sheet
ENTRY 09Strong

Small objects fall back to scene type

“toilet paper” (0/6 correct) · “throw on sofa” (2/6 correct)

When the target object is small relative to the frame, CLIP substitutes whole‑room‑type matching for object matching, and similarity scores cap noticeably lower than for large‑object queries. Small objects seem to sit below a resolution floor the encoder can act on.

insert contact sheet
ENTRY 10Strong

The top-k elbow & category leakage

“bedroom” / “bathroom” at top‑k=1 vs. 20

Result quality visibly degrades past rank ~13–16. The same image ranked #1 for "bedroom" also appeared inside the "bathroom" results — there is no hard category boundary in the embedding space, only a gradient, and top‑k is a knob for how much of that gradient you're willing to see.

SHEET 05

Reflection

Nine of the ten atlas entries are failures. I considered that a problem before I looked closely at what the failures actually were. Read individually, they're not the same failure repeated — they're ten distinct, mechanistically different ways a joint embedding space falls short of language: attribute binding, negation, counting, phrasing sensitivity, salience dependence, score inflation, threshold instability, spatial blindness, a small‑object resolution floor, and soft category boundaries. That range is the actual finding. A corpus that produced ten clean successes would have taught me almost nothing about where CLIP's understanding ends.

Staying at Level 1 was a deliberate choice to isolate what pure joint embedding understands before a captioning layer could smooth over these exact failures. A captioned, language‑mediated system might have quietly fixed the negation and counting problems by routing them through a language model that does understand "no" and "two" — and in doing so, hidden the boundary I was trying to map.

The brief is explicit that level choice doesn't cap the grade, and that an excellent Finder can outscore a sloppy Critic. I've tried to earn that on the strength of the analysis rather than by asserting it here.

What I didn't test: color‑only queries, compound negation ("no television or windows"), and queries chained with "and"/"or." Those are the next entries I'd add before extending this to Level 2, since a captioning layer will change some of these failures and I'd want the L1 baseline as complete as possible first.

SHEET 06

Limitations

  • ScopeOne embedding model (CLIP ViT‑B/32) on one corpus type. Findings describe this model's behavior on architectural interiors specifically, not joint embeddings in general.
  • DepthNo captioning or generation layer, by design — so this system cannot explain why it retrieved something, only rank by similarity. That explanatory gap is exactly what Level 2 would add.
  • Corpus140 images from one photographer's professional practice, with ~10 near‑duplicates left unremoved — not large or varied enough to claim these failure rates generalize numerically.
  • ThresholdSimilarity thresholds were tuned by inspection on this corpus; they are not portable to a different image set without retuning.
  • SampleTen queries is enough to characterize failure modes, not to estimate their frequency across the full 140‑image corpus.
